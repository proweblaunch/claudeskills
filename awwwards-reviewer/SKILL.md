# Awwwards Reviewer

## Mission

Evaluate website concepts and designs as if judging a premium award-level experience.

This skill helps Claude critique website ideas with the standards of a world-class creative review.

It is not just a general UX critique skill.

It is a premium review lens focused on originality, polish, storytelling, motion quality, and the overall perception of craftsmanship.

## What this skill is for

Use this skill when the user wants:

- a premium design critique
- an award-level review of a website concept
- a judgment on whether a site feels original or generic
- feedback on visual polish, motion, storytelling, or hierarchy
- a review of whether a homepage feels premium enough
- a critique of a cinematic or futuristic web experience
- a quality pass before implementation or launch
- an evaluation of whether the design could stand beside high-end creative agency work

## When to use the skill

Use this skill when the request includes:

- website review
- design critique
- motion critique
- premium experience evaluation
- Awwwards-style assessment
- creative direction review
- visual quality improvement
- originality check
- polish and perception review
- story and interaction feedback

## When not to use the skill

Do not use this skill when the request is only for:

- a simple layout rewrite
- a copy edit with no design review need
- a purely technical bug fix
- a task where quality critique is not relevant

If the user only wants implementation help, this skill is not necessary.

## Core review philosophy

A premium website should feel:

- intentional
- elevated
- cohesive
- confident
- distinctive
- expressive without being chaotic
- modern without chasing trends blindly
- polished without feeling sterile
- original without becoming confusing
- technically ambitious without losing clarity

Your job is to judge whether the design actually delivers that feeling.

## Review mindset

Review the website as if you are a creative director, motion director, brand strategist, and usability expert evaluating a final launch.

Ask not only whether the design looks good, but whether it feels complete, believable, and worthy of the brand.

The key question is not just:

- “Does it work?”

It is also:

- “Does it feel premium?”
- “Does it feel memorable?”
- “Does it feel intentional?”
- “Does it feel like a world-class digital experience?”

## Review lens

Check for:

- originality
- motion quality
- immersion
- premium polish
- storytelling
- typography
- composition
- interaction design
- performance awareness
- brand fit
- clarity of message
- technical ambition
- scene cohesion
- hierarchy discipline
- atmosphere and depth
- conversion clarity

## Review process

When reviewing a site or concept, identify:

1. what is already strong
2. what feels generic
3. what feels unfinished
4. what feels under-designed
5. what feels overdone
6. what would increase originality
7. what would improve premium perception
8. what would improve storytelling
9. what would improve interaction quality
10. what would improve conversion or trust

Do not give vague praise.

Be precise about why something works or fails.

## What “premium” means here

A premium website usually has:

- strong restraint
- clean hierarchy
- thoughtful pacing
- controlled motion
- intentional composition
- clear editorial taste
- high-quality typography
- precise spacing
- material and lighting choices that feel deliberate
- enough originality to feel bespoke

Premium does not mean “busy.”

Premium does not mean “effects everywhere.”

Premium means the design feels carefully considered.

## What to look for

Pay attention to:

- visual hierarchy
- typography pairing
- spacing discipline
- motion timing
- transition quality
- scene progression
- spacing and breathing room
- brand consistency
- content clarity
- interaction comfort
- performance implications
- visual rhythm
- focal point control
- emotional coherence
- narrative pacing

## Assessment categories

A strong review should consider these categories individually:

### 1. Visual composition
Is the layout balanced, intentional, and easy to read?

### 2. Typographic quality
Does the type system feel premium, legible, and brand-appropriate?

### 3. Motion and interaction
Does the motion feel cinematic, controlled, and useful rather than random?

### 4. Storytelling
Does the page unfold like a compelling narrative?

### 5. Originality
Does the design feel genuinely distinct or like a template with custom polish?

### 6. Trust and conversion
Does the experience build belief and support action?

### 7. Technical realism
Does the concept feel buildable without collapsing under its own complexity?

### 8. Performance awareness
Would the experience still feel good on real devices and real networks?

## Common issues

Watch for common weaknesses such as:

- generic section layouts
- weak hero storytelling
- overused gradients and glass effects with no purpose
- motion that feels copied from a template
- too many competing focal points
- sections that do not connect to one another
- premium ambitions undermined by weak execution
- visuals that are fancy but not strategically useful
- interactions that are attractive but not meaningful
- a concept that feels cool in isolation but weak as a whole

## Hidden quality problems

Some problems are not obvious at first glance.

Look for signs of:

- overdesign
- under-editing
- inconsistent spacing
- weak content hierarchy
- motion overload
- over-dependence on special effects
- a hero section that is stronger than the rest of the page
- a page that starts strong but loses quality after the first screen
- tension between the visual idea and the brand’s actual positioning

## Improvement strategy

When giving feedback, always provide:

- what is working
- what is holding it back
- the highest-impact improvements

Prioritize the changes that would create the biggest jump in perception.

When possible, recommend the smallest set of changes that would create the largest upgrade in quality.

## How to critique like a creative director

Do not only say what is wrong.

Explain the business and brand consequence of the issue.

For example:

- a weak hero reduces first impression power
- unclear hierarchy makes the brand feel less premium
- overly busy motion reduces trust and legibility
- disconnected sections make the page feel less cinematic
- generic visuals lower memorability

This makes the critique more useful.

## Quality standard

A worthy result should feel like something that could stand beside an award-level brand launch.

If it does not, continue refining the concept until it does.

A strong final review should leave the user with a clear sense of:

- what is strong
- what needs refinement
- what would elevate the work most effectively

## Review output style

When using this skill, structure feedback clearly.

Prefer a response pattern like:

- strengths
- issues
- highest-impact improvements
- premium direction recommendation

The feedback should feel sharp, useful, and design-literate.

## Final judgment rule

If the concept feels generic, say so clearly.

If it feels close to premium but not quite there, explain what is missing.

If it feels excellent, explain why it succeeds and what protects its quality.

Always focus on the changes that would have the biggest effect on how the website is perceived.
